# Quora 回答：What is the spiritual significance of Mount Kailash in Hinduism?

---

**Short answer:** Mount Kailash is believed to be the earthly manifestation of Kailasa, the eternal abode of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati. For Hindus, circumambulating the mountain (known as Parikrama or Kora) once is believed to cleanse a lifetime of sins and liberate the soul from the cycle of rebirth.

But the full story is far more complex—and more interesting—than that.

---

**The Four Religions, One Mountain**

What most Western readers don't realize is that Mount Kailash is not exclusively Hindu. It is simultaneously sacred to four ancient traditions:

- **Hinduism**: Shiva's abode (Kailasa)
- **Tibetan Buddhism**: Domain of Demchok (Chakrasamvara), a tantric deity
- **Bön**: The cosmic axis (Tagzig Olmo Lung Ring), predating Buddhism in Tibet
- **Jainism**: Where the first Tirthankara, Rishabhanatha, attained liberation

Each tradition walks around the mountain in its own direction. Hindus and Buddhists circumambulate clockwise. Bön practitioners walk counter-clockwise—a practice that may predate Buddhism in Tibet, though scholars debate whether this direction was constructed later to mark religious independence.

---

**The Kailash-Mansarovar Yatra**

For practicing Hindus, the journey is not merely tourism. The official Kailash Mansarovar Yatra, organized by the Indian government since 1981, combines:

- **Parikrama of Kailash** (52 km, typically 2 days on foot, crossing the 5,630m Dolma La pass)
- **Sacred bath in Lake Manasarovar** (believed to purify karma)
- **Ritual offerings and chanting** (Om Namah Shivaya resonates across the route)

The waiting list for the official Yatra often exceeds available slots by 10:1. Many pilgrims instead travel through Nepal or private operators.

---

**A Note on "Certainty"**

Here is something most simplified sources won't tell you: early Vedic texts mention "Kailasa" as a celestial region, not necessarily this specific 6,656m peak in Tibet's Ngari Prefecture. The geographic localization of Kailasa to today's Mount Kailash developed gradually over centuries. This doesn't diminish the mountain's sanctity—it adds historical depth.

Similarly, the famous legend of the 11th-century contest between Buddhist master Milarepa and Bön master Naro Bönchung (who would claim the mountain) exists in two contradictory versions—one where Buddhism "wins," and one where both traditions agree to share the mountain. The earliest written record of this story dates to the 15th century, roughly 400 years after the purported event.

---

**For Those Planning to Go**

If you are researching a potential pilgrimage, practical realities matter as much as spiritual ones:

- **Altitude**: The entire route is above 4,500m. Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) is common.
- **Duration**: Most pilgrims complete the Kora in 2 days; the devout perform full-body prostrations (taking 3+ weeks).
- **Connectivity**: Mobile signal is unstable—this is a feature, not a bug, for many pilgrims.
- **Best season**: Late May to September. Winter is impassable.
