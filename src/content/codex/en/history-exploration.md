## The First European Account (1715)

The first European known to have visited the Kailash region was **Ippolito Desideri** (1684-1733), an Italian Jesuit missionary who traveled through Tibet from 1715 to 1721. Desideri's detailed letters and manuscripts — rediscovered in the late 19th century — contain observations of Tibetan Buddhist practices around Kailash. His accounts were among the earliest Western descriptions of the kora ritual.

**Source:** Ippolito Desideri, *An Account of Tibet*, translated by Filippo de Filippi (Routledge, 1932).

## The British Surveyors (19th Century)

During the Great Trigonometrical Survey of India (1802-1871), British surveyors mapped the Himalayas with unprecedented precision. The Kailash region was particularly challenging due to its remote location and political sensitivities. **William Moorcroft** (1767-1825) was the first British explorer to reach Lake Manasarovar in 1812, providing the earliest European geographical description of the sacred lake.

**Source:** Charles Allen, *A Mountain in Tibet* (André Deutsch, 1982), chapters 2-4. This book is the authoritative account of Western exploration of Kailash.

## Sven Hedin (1907-1908)

The Swedish explorer **Sven Hedin** (1865-1952) conducted the most thorough early Western exploration of the Kailash region in 1907-1908. Hedin's expeditions produced:
- The first detailed maps of the Kailash-Manasarovar region
- Extensive meteorological and hydrological data
- Photographs of pilgrims on the kora (including the famous 1907 image documenting the massive 1906 Fire Horse Year pilgrimage)
- Attempts to measure Kailash's elevation (his figure of 6,714m was later revised)

Hedin made three separate attempts to reach the summit of Kailash but was unable to proceed beyond the lower slopes. He wrote extensively about the mountain in *Trans-Himalaya* (1909) and *Southern Tibet* (1917-1922).

⚠️ **Honest note:** Hedin was a product of colonial-era exploration. His writings contain cultural biases and geopolitical assumptions characteristic of his time. His scientific contributions stand independently of his political views.

**Source:** Sven Hedin, *Trans-Himalaya: Discoveries and Adventures in Tibet* (Macmillan, 1909-1913, 3 volumes).

## Giuseppe Tucci (1930s)

The Italian Tibetologist **Giuseppe Tucci** (1894-1984) was among the first Western scholars to approach Kailash as a subject of **academic study** rather than exploration. Tucci's expeditions in the 1930s documented Buddhist and Bön texts, art, and practices at Kailash. His work established the foundation for modern Western Tibetology.

**Source:** Giuseppe Tucci, *Tibetan Painted Scrolls* (Libreria dello Stato, 1949); *To Lhasa and Beyond* (Istituto Poligrafico dello Stato, 1956).

---

## Swami Pranavananda (1940s)

The Indian ascetic and explorer **Swami Pranavananda** (1896-1958) spent decades in the Kailash-Manasarovar region, completing **23 circumambulations** of Kailash and **25 circuits** of Lake Manasarovar. He lived continuously at Manasarovar for 16 months. His book, published with a foreword by Jawaharlal Nehru, remains one of the most detailed first-hand accounts of the region.

**Source:** Swami Pranavananda, *Kailas-Manasarovar* (S.P. League Ltd., Calcutta, 1949).

## Mid-20th Century: Closure and Reopening

After the 1950s, access to Kailash was severely restricted for foreign visitors. The region reopened gradually in the 1980s. The first post-reopening Western scholarly account was John Snelling's *The Sacred Mountain* (1983), which systematically documented pilgrimage traditions across all four religions.

**Source:** John Snelling, *The Sacred Mountain: Travellers and Pilgrims at Mount Kailas in Western Tibet* (East-West Publications, 1983; revised 1990).
