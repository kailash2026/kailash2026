## Geography and Climate

Mount Kailash is located in **Ngari Prefecture**, western Tibet Autonomous Region, at approximately 31°04′N, 81°18′E. The region sits on the **Tibetan Plateau**, the world's highest and largest plateau, with an average elevation exceeding 4,500 meters.

### Climate Characteristics

- **High-altitude cold desert climate** (Köppen classification: ETH)
- **Annual precipitation**: 200-300mm, mostly as snow
- **Temperature range**: Summer highs of 10-15°C (50-59°F); winter lows of -30°C (-22°F)
- **Diurnal temperature variation**: 15-20°C difference between day and night is common
- **Intense solar radiation** due to high altitude and thin atmosphere
- **Strong winds**: Prevailing westerlies, especially severe at passes above 5,000m

### Pilgrimage Season (May-October)

The practical window for the kora is limited to May through October. Even during summer, snowstorms can occur at high passes. The Drolma La pass (5,630m) is typically impassable from November through April.

## Hydrology: The Four Rivers

The Kailash region is the source of four major Asian rivers, a geographical fact that significantly contributes to the mountain's sacred status:

| River | Tibetan Name | Animal Symbol | Flows To |
|-------|-------------|:---:|----------|
| Indus | 狮泉河 (Sengge Tsangpo) | Lion | Arabian Sea |
| Sutlej | 象泉河 (Langchen Tsangpo) | Elephant | Indus River |
| Brahmaputra | 马泉河 (Tamchok Tsangpo) | Horse | Bay of Bengal |
| Karnali (Ganges system) | 孔雀河 (Mabja Tsangpo) | Peacock | Ganges River |

⚠️ **Scientific note:** While all four rivers originate in the **general vicinity** of Kailash (within approximately 100 km), they do not all originate from a single point on the mountain itself. The traditional belief that four rivers flow directly from Kailash's four sides is a **religious concept**, not a precise hydrological description. Modern hydrology provides a more nuanced picture of the actual watershed.

The region also includes two major lakes:
- **Lake Manasarovar** (玛旁雍错): Freshwater, 4,588m elevation, considered sacred in Hinduism and Buddhism
- **Lake Rakshastal** (拉昂错): Saltwater, 4,573m elevation, located just west of Manasarovar

## Flora

The Kailash region is characterized by **alpine steppe and cold desert vegetation**. Plant life is sparse at high elevations:

- **Alpine grasslands** (4,500-5,000m): Dominated by *Stipa* and *Kobresia* species
- **Subnival zone** (above 5,000m): Limited to lichens and mosses on rocks
- **No trees**: The region lies above the treeline (approximately 4,200m in this latitude)
- **Medicinal plants**: *Rhodiola* (红景天), used in traditional Tibetan medicine for altitude adaptation, grows in the region

## Fauna

Despite the harsh environment, the Kailash region supports adapted wildlife:

- **Wild ungulates**: Tibetan wild ass (*kiang*, *Equus kiang*), Tibetan gazelle (*goa*, *Procapra picticaudata*), blue sheep (*bharal*, *Pseudois nayaur*)
- **Predators**: Snow leopard (*Panthera uncia*), Tibetan wolf (*Canis lupus chanco*), Tibetan fox (*Vulpes ferrilata*)
- **Birds**: Himalayan griffon vulture, black-necked crane, Tibetan snowcock
- **Domestic animals**: Yak and dzo (yak-cattle hybrid) are essential to local pastoral economy

The snow leopard, classified as **Vulnerable** by the IUCN, is the region's most iconic predator. It is rarely seen by pilgrims but holds cultural significance in Tibetan folklore.

## Conservation Status

The Kailash region is not within a formally designated national park, though parts of the broader area fall within the **Changtang Nature Reserve**. Key conservation challenges include:

- **Climate change**: The Tibetan Plateau is warming at approximately twice the global average rate, causing glacial retreat
- **Waste management**: Increasing pilgrim numbers generate solid waste along the kora route
- **Grassland degradation**: Overgrazing and climate factors affect traditional pastoral lands
